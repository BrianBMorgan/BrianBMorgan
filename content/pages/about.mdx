I started on the engineering side — shipping front-ends for teams that had strong ideas and thin design support. Filling that gap became the job. Over the years it grew into a practice that runs on three lanes: building products, producing the events that launch them, and drawing the identities that hold everything together.

The through-line is endurance. I'd rather ship one system a team still uses in five years than ten screens they redesign in one. That bias shows up in how I work: tokens before taste, documentation as part of the deliverable, and a strong preference for boring technology that outlives its trend cycle.

## How I work

- **Small teams, direct lines.** I work best embedded with the people who own the problem — no layers, no telephone.
- **Working software over theater.** Early and often beats polished and late. The first prototype usually lands inside week one.
- **The last five percent.** Easing curves, empty states, error copy — the details nobody budgets for are the ones people remember.

## Now

I take on a handful of projects each quarter from my studio in Portland. Between engagements I write, speak, and keep a workshop habit — the [thought leadership](/thought-leadership) section is where that thinking lands.
