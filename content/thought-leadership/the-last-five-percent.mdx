Every project plan allocates zero hours for the things users mention first. The easing on a drawer. The empty state that explains instead of apologizes. Error copy written by someone who imagined an actual person reading it. This is the last five percent, and it is where "works" becomes "considered."

## Why it gets cut

The last five percent has no ticket. It is not a feature, so it cannot be prioritized against features and it loses to all of them. By the time it could be scheduled, the budget is spent and the team is tired.

The only way I have found to protect it is to refuse the framing. The drawer's easing is not polish on the drawer — it *is* the drawer. An interface with unconsidered details is not 95% done; it is done, and it is mediocre.

## Budget it like load-bearing work

- Put a named line in the estimate: motion, empty states, error language. Money makes it real.
- Do it during, not after. The last five percent done last costs triple, because now it fights settled code.
- Assign taste, not tasks. One person owns how the whole thing feels; committees produce the average.

Twelve years in, I have never had a client point at the architecture diagram in a retrospective. They point at the moment the interface did something quietly right. That moment was the budget line everyone tried to cut.
