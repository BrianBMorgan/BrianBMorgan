Ask a team to show you their brand and they open a slide of colors. Ask them to change a color and the estimate is three weeks. The gap between those two answers is the cost of choosing taste before tokens.

## Storage before selection

A token is just a decision with an address. `--accent-primary` is not a color; it is the *place a color lives*, which means the color can move without anyone chasing it through 400 files. The same logic runs anywhere decisions accumulate — type scales, spacing grids, even run-of-show templates for a recurring event.

The order matters more than the values:

1. Name the roles — surface, ink, accent, warning — before picking what fills them.
2. Fill the roles with today's taste.
3. Let every surface reference the role, never the value.

Do it in that order and taste stays cheap to change forever. Do it backwards and every aesthetic opinion calcifies into infrastructure.

## The redesign test

You know the sequencing worked when the rebrand ships as a token diff. I have watched a marketing site absorb a full palette change in an afternoon — layout untouched, components untouched, 40 lines of CSS variables. The design system did not resist the redesign; it was the reason the redesign was affordable.

Taste is the part everyone sees. Tokens are the reason it can keep changing. Build the vault before you argue about what goes in it.
