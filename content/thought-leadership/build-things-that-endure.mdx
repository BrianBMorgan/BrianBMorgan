Most software is demolished, not retired. A product gets rebuilt because nobody can safely change it, a brand gets redesigned because nobody wrote down why it looked that way, an event gets forgotten because the thinking lived in one producer's head. None of that is fate. It is a set of choices, and they get made early.

## Endurance is a sequencing problem

Teams under deadline choose taste first — the hero image, the launch page, the demo. Taste is visible and endurance is not, so endurance loses. The fix is not more discipline; it is a different order of operations.

Write the decision down before you use it. A color becomes a token before it becomes a button. A stage moment becomes a run-of-show line before it becomes a rehearsal. The artifact that captures the decision is the thing that survives the person who made it.

> Ship the system that makes the tenth project cheap, not the demo that makes the first one impressive.

## Three tests

I run every deliverable against the same three questions:

1. **Can someone I never met extend this?** If it takes a meeting with me, it isn't done.
2. **What breaks if the team doubles?** Whatever breaks is the part I under-documented.
3. **Will the third redesign keep it?** Logos change and palettes drift, but a sound information architecture — of a codebase, a brand, or a program — tends to survive.

Endurance is not conservatism. It is respect for the people who inherit your work — including the future version of you who has forgotten all of it.
